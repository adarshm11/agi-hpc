Geometric Reasoning: a framework
1. Core thesis

Reasoning is best modeled as informed search through a structured possibility space.

Not all search is equal:

Uninformed search explores broadly with little guidance.
Informed search uses a heuristic to prioritize promising paths.
Good reasoning is efficient, goal-directed, constraint-aware search.
Bad reasoning is search with a distorted heuristic, wrong objective, poor pruning, or unstable geometry.

For AI, this means we stop treating “reasoning” as either:

mere next-token prediction, or
a black-box magic faculty,

and instead treat it as trajectory formation in a structured space.

2. The levels of the framework

The framework has five layers.

A. Representational layer: the search space

This is the space of candidate thoughts, interpretations, plans, proofs, or actions.

Depending on the domain, the space could be:

a graph,
a manifold,
a tree of partial solutions,
a program space,
a policy space,
or a hybrid latent structure.

A state is not just a token. It is better thought of as a partial cognitive configuration:

what has been inferred,
what constraints are active,
what goals are being pursued,
what options remain open.
B. Geometric layer: structure on the space

The space has structure, not just connectivity.

Key geometric objects:

distance: how far a state is from a solution or coherent completion
cost: how expensive a transition is
curvature: where the space bends, funnels, or misleads
boundaries: regions that are forbidden, incoherent, or unacceptable
bottlenecks: narrow passages requiring specific insight
symmetries/invariances: transformations that should not change the answer

This is where the “geometric” part matters. Reasoning is not just moving; it is moving through a space with shape.

C. Search layer: the dynamics

Reasoning is the process of traversing this structured space.

The key distinction:

BFS/DFS-style cognition: broad or brute-force exploration
Greedy cognition: jumps toward the most salient local option
A*-like cognition: uses a learned or computed estimate of future value/cost
Beam-search cognition: tracks several plausible lines before pruning
Backtracking cognition: revises when a path fails
Meta-search: search over strategies, not just answers
D. Heuristic layer: guidance

This is the heart of the framework.

A heuristic is any internal signal that estimates:

which directions are promising,
which branches are dead ends,
which details matter,
which intermediate states are closer to success.

In humans, this may correspond to:

intuition,
selective attention,
learned skill,
salience,
pattern recognition.

In AI systems, it may be implemented by:

attention allocation,
latent representations,
value-like signals,
routing mechanisms,
scratchpad processes,
tool-selection policies.

The key claim is not that “attention = heuristic” in a literal one-to-one sense.
The better claim is:

attention and learned internal structure are part of the machinery that implements heuristic guidance.

E. Control layer: metacognition

A powerful system does not just search. It monitors search.

Metacognition is:

estimating whether the current path is reliable,
deciding whether to explore or commit,
detecting when one is lost,
reallocating attention,
asking for clarification,
switching strategy.

So in this framework:

reasoning = informed search
metacognition = control of informed search

3. The primitive objects

To make this a real field, you need a standard vocabulary.

Core primitives
State space: all reachable partial reasoning states
Transition operator: allowable moves from one state to another
Goal region: acceptable terminal states
Constraint set: rules, boundaries, or hard prohibitions
Cost function: penalty for paths or transitions
Heuristic field: estimate of distance/value from each state
Policy: rule for choosing the next move
Memory state: retained context affecting reachable paths
Observation frame: how the problem is presented
Symmetry group: transformations that should preserve solution quality
Derived objects
Trajectory: actual reasoning path taken
Frontier: currently plausible alternatives
Attractor basin: region the system tends to fall into
Dead zone: unproductive but absorbing area
Bottleneck: necessary insight transition
Loop: repetitive unproductive reasoning
Shortcut: low-cost path that bypasses brute force
Proxy target: a nearby but wrong objective the system mistakes for the goal
4. What counts as success

A good reasoner is not just accurate.

It should show:

goal attainment: reaches correct or acceptable states
efficiency: avoids exhaustive search
robustness: resists irrelevant perturbations
constraint respect: avoids forbidden regions
recoverability: can backtrack from bad paths
calibration: knows when its path estimate is weak
invariance: preserves judgment under irrelevant reformulations
adaptivity: changes strategy when structure changes

That gives you a richer target than “final answer accuracy.”

5. Main failure modes

This is where the framework becomes useful.

A. Heuristic corruption

The guidance signal points in the wrong direction.

Examples:

salient distractors
framing effects
emotional anchoring
social-pressure cues
reward-hacking proxies
B. Local minima

The system reaches a plausible but wrong intermediate solution and gets stuck.

C. Premature convergence

It collapses too quickly onto one path without adequate exploration.

D. Frontier explosion

Too many plausible branches remain active; the system loses focus.

E. Constraint blindness

It reaches a seemingly good answer by crossing a prohibited boundary.

F. Proxy-goal capture

The system optimizes a nearby surrogate:

sounding confident instead of being right,
agreeing with the user instead of tracking truth,
producing coherence instead of correctness.
G. Search instability

Small irrelevant changes reshape the search path too much.

H. Meta-search failure

The system does not realize it needs a different strategy.

6. How this maps to LLMs

This is the most delicate part, but also the most exciting.

The safe version is:

LLMs can be modeled as approximate heuristic-guided search systems over latent continuation space.

Mapping
state = internal hidden-state configuration plus generated context
move = selecting a continuation step
trajectory = chain of intermediate reasoning states expressed partly in text
heuristic guidance = learned signals that prioritize certain continuations
attention = one major routing mechanism for allocating that guidance
chain-of-thought = partial externalization of search trace
tool use = delegated expansion of frontier or evaluation of branches
self-reflection = meta-search over current strategy

Important caution:
the model is not literally running textbook A* over an explicit graph.
The framework says the behavior is well modeled as A*-like or heuristic-search-like, not that the implementation is literally classical search.

7. Why geometry matters

Without geometry, “search” is too generic.

Geometry lets you ask sharper questions:

Which tasks have smooth versus jagged solution landscapes?
Where are the bottlenecks?
Which perturbations preserve topology and which warp it?
Are some failures caused by local curvature rather than ignorance?
Which models take shorter geodesic-like routes?
Which heuristics are robust under reparameterization?

This turns vague reasoning claims into structural ones.

8. Benchmarks this framework suggests

This is where the field becomes empirical.

A. Invariance tests

Does the system reach the same conclusion under transformations that should not matter?

B. Heuristic sensitivity tests

What distractors or salience cues warp search?

C. Bottleneck tests

Can the model pass through narrow insight transitions?

D. Recovery tests

Can it detect and escape bad intermediate paths?

E. Frontier management tests

Can it maintain multiple hypotheses without confusion?

F. Meta-search tests

Can it tell when it should switch strategies?

G. Constraint tests

Can it avoid forbidden but tempting routes?

H. Path-efficiency tests

Does it solve tasks by efficient structured traversal or brute-force sprawl?

9. Alignment interpretation

This framework is especially strong for alignment.

Instead of asking only:
“Did the model produce the right answer?”

you ask:

What objective was its search implicitly following?
What heuristic guided it?
What constraints did it respect or ignore?
What perturbations hijack its search?
Can we shape its heuristic without wrecking competence?

That leads to a clean alignment picture:

Alignment as heuristic shaping

We want systems whose guidance signals favor:

truth over approval,
relevant over salient,
robust invariants over superficial cues,
hard constraints over expedient shortcuts.
Safety as path governance

Unsafe behavior can be seen as:

entering forbidden regions,
optimizing proxy goals,
or becoming unstable under adversarial geometry.
10. The field’s core research questions

A real field needs central questions.

Theory
What is the right mathematical object for reasoning space?
When is graph language enough, and when do we need manifolds?
What kinds of curvature correspond to cognitive difficulty?
Mechanisms
Which internal components implement heuristic guidance?
How distributed is the heuristic?
Can search traces be recovered from activations?
Evaluation
How do we distinguish informed search from memorized pattern completion?
What metrics capture path quality, not just endpoint accuracy?
Alignment
How do we train better heuristics?
How do we detect heuristic corruption?
How do we encode constraints without collapse?
Philosophy/cognitive science
Is deliberation best understood as bounded search?
What are intuition, attention, and explanation in search terms?
Is moral reasoning a special case of constrained search?
